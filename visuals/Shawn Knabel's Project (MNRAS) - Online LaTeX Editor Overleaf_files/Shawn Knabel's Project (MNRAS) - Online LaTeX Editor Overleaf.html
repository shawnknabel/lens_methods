With three completely independent
techniques to identify strong
gravitational lenses in the same three
fields on the sky (Figure
\ref{fig:gama_regions}), we have an
opportunity to test how well different
identification techniques can be
calibrated against one another,
discover implicit selection effects in
each, and .
\section{Data and Observations}
In order to obtain a valid comparison
of the three techniques, the
catalogues of candidate lenses
obtained by mixed-spectroscopy
\cite{Holwerda15}, machine-learning
\cite{Petrillo18}, and GalaxyZoo
(Kelvin et al. {\em in prep.}) were
cut to represent only those candidates
identified within the equatorial GAMA
regions G09, G12, and G15, which are
entirely contained within the scope of
KiDS (Figure \ref{fig:gama_regions}).
This cut resulted in usable catalogues
consisting of 85
spectroscopically-identified lens
candidates, 421 candidates identified
by machine learning, and a misleading
total of 12934 GalaxyZoo candidates.
Further cuts to the catalogues were
then made to account for ambiguity
introduced by subjective visual
scoring in both the machine-learning
and citizen-science techniques. %We
took a threshold of 20 percent to have
a more reasonable catalogue.
